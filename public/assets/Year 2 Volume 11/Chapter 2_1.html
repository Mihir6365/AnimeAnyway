Part 1

After travelling on the highway for about two hours, the mountains completely dominated the view outside the window.

The bus halted at a different place compared to last year, and the students started to disembark.

The space in front of the main entrance, where the buses were parked, was surprisingly spacious.

The building designated for our camp stay resembled a traditional inn with a rich history.

As explained by the school, it was originally constructed as a lodging facility and activity center during the economic boom period.

Inside the building, there were dedicated classrooms for each activity.

This explained the numerous experiential games mentioned in the earlier printout.

"Gather in your assigned groups. For the next three days, follow your leader's instructions, engage in discussions, and get along with everyone."

We were instructed.

The 20 third-year students who were appointed as group leaders spread out.

Right in front of me was Kiryuuin, her hands tucked into her jersey jacket pockets.

"Well, see you later, Kiyotaka."

Kei said, hesitantly moving away.

I watched Kei leave for a moment before approaching Kiryuuin.

"I look forward to working with you for the next three days, Kiryuuin-senpai."

"Count on it."

Kiryuuin's group, Group 20, included a total of 16 first and second-year members.

First-year Students

Class A: Toyohashi Goro, Kosumi Dan

Class B: Yanagi Yasuhisa, Eikura Mani

Class C: Tsubaki Sakurako, Shintoku Taro

Class D: Obokata Yukiki, Jute Misora.


Second-year Students

Class A: Hashimoto Masayoshi, Yamamura Miki, Morishita Ai

Class B: Ayanokoji Kiyotaka, Nishimura Ryūko

Class C: Oda Takumi, Shiina Hiyori

Class D: Hatsukawa Maho.

Kiryuuin was our group leader.

The group seemed to consist of students who were good at sports and those who excelled in academics.

Achieving such a balance in a competitive setting would be tough, but since this was a casual exchange meeting centered around games, it worked out.

Among the second-year students, I recognized many faces, but among the first-year students, the only one I knew was Tsubaki.

This highlighted the importance of holding an exchange meeting like this.

“Hey, I never thought I’d end up teaming with you like this.”

Hashimoto remarked as the group gathered, approaching me casually.

“I agree.”

Just recently, I had many discussions with Hashimoto and the others, and it felt strange that the four of us from that day were now in the same group.

“I’m both happy and disappointed. I wish we could’ve been together in some high-stakes special exam.”

Hashimoto added, indicating he had high expectations for me.

I hadn’t mentioned if I could meet those expectations yet, but I left it at that.

“Even if it's just an exchange meeting, I’d be happy to earn some decent money if I can come out on top. At the very least, exchanging contact info with the first-years is essential. I’ll invite you to join the group later.”

It was helpful to have someone willing to take on the coordinating role—a time-consuming task.

“I might delete your contact next month, though.” I joked.

“Hey, hey, stop with the unfunny Morishita-like jokes.” Hashimoto replied.

Although I made the joke, it did sound a bit like something Morishita would say. I wondered if his peculiar personality was rubbing off on me.

Just then, a gentle voice interrupted my thoughts.

“Good morning, Ayanokoji-kun.”

Hiyori greeted as she approached the group.

“Good morning. I’ll be counting on you starting today. It’s reassuring to have you here.”

“I feel the same. I was relieved to know we’re in the same group.”

Hiyori seemed to be universally accepted, unlike me, but she viewed the world differently from most.

Having a dependable friend was genuinely comforting.

“Hashimoto-kun, I look forward to working with you as well.”

Hiyori added, giving a slight bow.

“I always welcome cute girls. But you know, you two look good together standing side by side.”

“What do you mean?”

“Don’t take this the wrong way, but I think you two look more natural together than when you’re with Karuizawa.”

Perhaps our shared interest in reading made it seem different from my relationship with Kei, but there was no need to take Hashimoto’s words too seriously.

He had already shifted his focus to the gathered group.

Kiryuuin, ignoring the group, stared at the winter mountains, prompting Hashimoto to take charge.

“Um, is that everyone? Ah, no, are we one short? One, two, three—”

Hashimoto quickly counted.

“Fifteen, and sixteen including me. Looks like we’re still one short.”

One short? I thought everyone was here. Was I mistaken?

“There are 17 of us here. Yamamura Miki is also here.” I pointed out.

“Oh, really, everyone’s here... Sorry, Yamamura.”

Hashimoto said, hastily correcting himself.

“No... I’m sorry.”

Yamamura apologized, despite just being overlooked. Her inconspicuousness was evident, being unnoticed by Kiryuuin and overlooked by her classmate Hashimoto, and it seemed even more pronounced recently.

However, once I recognized Yamamura’s presence, it became oddly more noticeable to me, likely because I hadn't sensed her as much before.

This might just be my perspective, though.

When I asked Hiyori about Yamamura, she mentioned she had never really talked to her before, so I decided to introduce them.

“We seem to be running into each other a lot lately.”

“Yes, that's true. I look forward to...”

“Nice to meet you, Yamamura-san.”

Hiyori greeted her with a warm smile, which made Yamamura stiffen slightly.

“Ah, yes. You're Shiina-san, right...?”

Yamamura greeted Hiyori hesitantly, seeming a bit uneasy.

“Oh? Do you have something you want to ask me?”

“Ah—that... you're completely different from what I thought...”

Yamamura murmured.

“Me?”

Hiyori tilted her head in confusion.

“I thought you were more... indifferent...”

Yamamura admitted, speaking softly.

Yamamura, who observed people from a distance, had seen Hiyori differently.

I had felt the same before getting to know her.

“I'm sorry. I'm not good at talking to people, so I might’ve said something rude...”

“It's totally fine. I'm not good at talking to people either, so we're similar.”

Hiyori reassured her.

“Is that... so?”

Yamamura responded, though her eyes showed doubt.

“You don’t see it? If so, I think it’s thanks to Ayanokoji-kun.”

“Ayanokoji-kun...?” Yamamura echoed, puzzled.

“Yes. Even though I’m not good at it, I’ve come to love talking with my friends. So I think you will surely come to love talking too.”

Hiyori said, holding Yamamura’s hand.

Hiyori’s claim that it was thanks to me was an exaggeration, but I hoped Yamamura would feel the same way eventually.

With that, all the members of the Kiryuuin group were present.

“Ayanokoji Kiyotaka. Nice to see you.”

Morishita greeted me, using my full name without any honorifics but politely.

“Nice to see you too.”

“You’re—um... Shiina Hiyori, right? I’m Morishita Ai. Hello, hello.”

She said, bowing slightly.

“I’m Shiina. Nice to meet you, Morishita-san.”

Starting with Yamamura, the second-year students exchanged light greetings, then began greeting the first-year students, who were nervously gathered.

Kiryuuin, who had been waiting without intervening, finally spoke up.

“Now that the greetings are over, let’s have lunch. Let’s disband for now.”

“Wait a minute, Kiryuuin-senpai. Wouldn’t it be better for us to have lunch together to strengthen the camaraderie of the group?”

Indeed, in that situation, Hashimoto’s suggestion was reasonable.

Most groups seemed to be starting to act as a unit.

“Then I’ll leave it to you.”

Kiryuuin said, indicating she wouldn’t be joining us.

She then left the group and disappeared into the inn alone.

“Man, seriously? We've got quite a leader here.”

Hashimoto sighed, exasperated.

“We can ignore her. I agree with the group lunch.”

“That’s right. She said she’d leave it to us, so there’s little reason to disband here.”

Hashimoto agreed, deciding it was best not to overthink it.

The first-year students looked confused, so he started acting quickly.

Even if some first-years were reluctant to eat with their seniors, objections were unlikely except from strong personalities like Housen.

“Wait a minute, hey! Koenji!”

Behind Hashimoto, who was explaining things to the first-year students, there was a small issue brewing in another group nearby.

Koenji, who was assigned to the sixth group, had wandered off without following the leader's instructions.

The other second-year students in Koenji's group, familiar with his behaviour, didn't say anything to the confused first-year students.

Even Inogashira, a classmate, looked worried but eventually let it go.

For a moment, she and I made eye contact before she turned back to listen to her angry leader.

“What’s Koenji-kun doing?”

Hiyori wondered aloud, watching Koenji walk away.

“He always does his own thing. I doubt he’ll come back.”

“Really?”

“Yeah, Koenji Rokusuke doesn’t work well in groups. We’re used to it. My condolences.”

Morishita added, clasping her hands together as if praying for the sixth group, already struggling with coordination.

“If you were in the same group, would you have stopped him, Ayanokoji?”

“Being in the same class, I know it’s pointless to stop him. I’d just ignore it.”

If anyone could have stopped him by calling out, it wouldn't have been an issue.

“Alright, the first-years are ready. Let’s go.”

Hashimoto instructed, and our group started moving without our leader.

As we entered the inn, a slightly damp smell greeted us.

It didn’t seem like a place that was used often.

We walked in a line towards the cafeteria.

With Kiryuuin absent, Hashimoto naturally took on the leadership role.

During our lively group lunch, he led the conversation, engaging the hesitant first-year students and the quieter ones, boosting their spirits without any silly antics, and skillfully involving everyone in the discussion.

For someone like me, who usually just listened, he was a lifesaver.

“Um... Hashimoto-senpai. According to the rules of this exchange meeting, we don’t all need to be present for the games, right?” a first-year asked.

“Yeah. Each game requires up to five participants, and the same person can play multiple times. It’s pretty flexible.”

We only needed the required number of participants plus the leader to be present at the designated times.

“Looking at Kiryuuin-senpai, it seems like she’s not interested in the exchange meeting. We can probably do whatever we want... but I wish she’d at least told us her plans.” another student added.

Since the leader was responsible for selecting players, Kiryuuin was supposed to decide once the game details were announced.

Hashimoto seemed annoyed that Kiryuuin hadn’t asked anyone about their strengths or skills.

“For now, all we can do is try our best.”

“Kiryuuin-senpai is amazing, isn’t she? Maybe she already knows about us?”

Jitsute, a first-year from Class D, asked Hashimoto.

Even if she didn’t know them personally, Kiryuuin's exceptional abilities were well-known.

“That’s not possible. There’s no way she could know who in this group is good at something like flower arranging.”

Hashimoto replied skeptically. At this point, no one knew each other’s strengths and weaknesses.

“There’s a reason I suggested we all eat together. Let’s rate our confidence in the game contents written on the printout on a scale from one to five. One means you’re not confident.”

It was a simple yet necessary decision that the leader should have initiated.

Everyone used their phones to rate their abilities for the various games.

However, many activities were unusual and unfamiliar.

If someone had never experienced an activity before, they could only rate it a one, and even if they felt they might be good at it, the highest rating they gave was a two.

Moreover, there was no place to practice many of the activities in advance, making things requiring creativity particularly challenging.

As they ate, everyone filled out their ratings.

Some students finished eating by the time they were done.

This way, we could gather data to guide our decisions.

Hashimoto immediately shared the results in the group chat he created.

“...This isn't going to work.”

Hashimoto said after reviewing the data.

Almost all students had rated their confidence as one or two for most games, with hardly any fours or fives.

Hashimoto seemed to lose hope of winning.

“Maybe we should just give up on the exchange meeting and just play around.”

But it was too early to make that judgement.

This phenomenon was likely happening in other groups as well.

“I feel like there are fewer groups that are serious about it... Well, for now, I’ll show this information to Kiryuuin-senpai and let her decide on the plan.”

In the end, everything depended on Kiryuuin’s motivation.

If she was driven, the underclassmen would follow her lead.

If she wasn’t, she’d just participate casually and relax at the training camp.

Personally, I preferred taking it easy.
